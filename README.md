
This analysis begins the extension of urban scaling laws to the national level. The book "Scale" by Geoffrey West and its corresponding paper establish approximate universal scaling laws of cities. Drawing from an analogy to Kleiber's Law in biology that relates mass to metabolism in organisms, West demonstrates that contrary to linear inuition, the socioeconomic variables of a city such as the number of new patents, total wages, and crime, scale exponentially relative to population while infrastructural (or analogically metabolic) variables, such as the number of gas stations, length of electical cables, and road surface scale logarithmically. These relationships have broad and deep consequences for economics, urban design, and sustainability. This analysis examines corresponding relationships at the national and global level.
